What is HDR?

HDR, or High Dynamic Range, is a technology used in imaging and video that captures and displays a wider range of brightness and color than standard formats. It allows for deeper blacks, brighter highlights, and more detail in both dark and light areas of an image. This results in more lifelike visuals, especially noticeable in scenes with strong contrasts like sunsets or night shots with bright lights. HDR is commonly used in photography, film, and modern displays such as TVs, monitors, and smartphones.

Benefits of HDR

  • Way better image quality: Greater detail in shadows and highlights, richer colors.
  • Higher engagement: More immersive and visually appealing content keeps viewersâ€™ attention longer.
  • Improved realism: Scenes look more natural, closer to how the human eye perceives light.
  • Enhanced contrast: Deep blacks and bright highlights improve visual impact.
  • Better for storytelling: Ideal for dramatic lighting, sci-fi, nature, and high-end production value.
  • Future-proofing: HDR is becoming a standard across devices and platforms.
  • Compatibility with modern devices: Most new TVs, smartphones, and VR headsets support HDR.

Drawbacks of HDR

  • Slower production pipeline: Requires more processing time and heavier file formats.
  • Need for HDR-capable source material: Raw HDR footage or 32-bit images are essential to get real benefits.
  • Limited support across platforms: Not all browsers, apps, or displays handle HDR properly yet.
  • Complex post-production: Requires color grading with HDR scopes and proper tone mapping.
  • Larger file sizes: Increases storage and bandwidth requirements.
  • Steeper learning curve: Teams need to adapt to new workflows, software, and export settings.
